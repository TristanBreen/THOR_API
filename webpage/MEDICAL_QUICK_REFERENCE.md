| Metric | What It Means | Action If High | Action If Low |
|--------|---------------|----------------|---------------|

## Key Thresholds & Interpretations

### Food Impact
- **>40 sec difference** = Clinically significant trigger/protector
- **>60% seizures with food** = Strong food trigger pattern
- **>20 sec difference** = Notable but may be random variation
- **<10 sec difference** = Likely not clinically significant

### Menstrual Correlation
- **>3:1 ratio** (period:non-period seizures) = Catamenial pattern â†’ HRT evaluation
- **70%+ period seizures** = Strong hormonal influence â†’ Perimenstrual prophylaxis
- **25-30% period seizures** = Expected rate for cyclical disorders
- **<20% period seizures** = Minimal hormonal influence

### Pain-Seizure Correlation
- **>75% correlation** = Probable PNES/psychogenic component
- **50-75% correlation** = Mixed etiology (epilepsy + stress)
- **25-50% correlation** = Weak pain-seizure link
- **<25% correlation** = Pain likely incidental

### Inter-Seizure Intervals
- **Avg <12h** = Cluster seizures/refractory, urgent med adjustment
- **Avg 12h-7d** = Active epilepsy, monitor closely
- **Avg 7-30d** = Adequate control, continue current therapy
- **Avg >30d** = Excellent control, consider dose reduction

### Duration Variability (Coefficient of Variation)
- **>100%** = Highly inconsistent â†’ PNES workup
- **50-100%** = Variable â†’ Mixed epilepsy/PNES possible
- **20-50%** = Moderate consistency â†’ Typical epilepsy
- **<20%** = Highly consistent â†’ Pure epilepsy pattern

## Common Patterns & What To Do

### Pattern 1: Food Trigger + High Pain Correlation
**Interpretation**: Likely PNES triggered by anticipatory food anxiety
**Action**: 
1. Psychiatric evaluation for anxiety, trauma
2. Video-EEG (may show non-epileptic activity)
3. Dietary reassurance/counseling
4. Consider reducing anti-seizure meds

---

### Pattern 2: Clear Catamenial + Normal Pain
**Interpretation**: Hormonal epilepsy, not psychogenic
**Action**:
1. Hormone panel (estradiol, progesterone levels)
2. Refer to reproductive endocrinology
3. Trial of perimenstrual prophylaxis (extra medication 3 days before/during/after period)
4. Consider oral contraceptives (if appropriate for patient)

---

### Pattern 3: Low Food Impact + Low Pain + Increasing Duration + Short Intervals
**Interpretation**: Worsening generalized epilepsy
**Action**:
1. STAT: Check anti-seizure medication levels
2. Order routine EEG
3. Assess medication compliance
4. Consider medication increase or add second agent
5. Rule out secondary causes (infection, metabolic, tumor)

---

### Pattern 4: Long Intervals + Decreasing Duration Trend
**Interpretation**: Current therapy likely effective
**Action**:
1. Continue current medication regimen
2. Reinforce compliance
3. Assess lifestyle changes (stress reduction, sleep improvement)
4. Monthly follow-up to ensure trend continues

---

### Pattern 5: Highly Variable Intervals + High Duration CV + High Pain Correlation
**Interpretation**: PNES highly likely
**Action**:
1. Priority psychiatric referral
2. Video-EEG monitoring (diagnostic)
3. Counseling about PNES (explain non-epileptic nature)
4. Gradual medication reduction under supervision
5. Psychological therapy initiation

## Questions to Ask Patient After Reviewing Insights

### Food-Related
- "Have you noticed seizures happen more after eating certain foods?"
- "Do you worry about food causing seizures?"
- "Any GI symptoms (nausea, reflux) before seizures?"

### Period-Related
- "Do seizures cluster around your period?"
- "Any pattern based on your cycle?"
- "Worse stress/anxiety at certain times of month?"

### Pain-Related
- "When do you experience pain - just before, during, or after seizures?"
- "What situations trigger both pain AND seizures?"
- "Any triggers like stress, arguments, or emotional events?"

### Interval-Related
- "Can you predict when seizures might occur?"
- "Do they seem to come in clusters (multiple close together)?"
- "Any weeks/months completely seizure-free?"

## Medication Adjustments Based on Insights

### If Interval Average Decreasing
- Check drug level (therapeutic vs subtherapeutic)
- If subtherapeutic â†’ Increase dose
- If therapeutic but worsening â†’ Consider:
  - Add second agent
  - Switch to different medication
  - Rule out medical triggers

### If Duration Trend Increasing
- Same as above (medication likely insufficient)
- Also assess: compliance, absorption, drug interactions

### If Pain Correlation High (>75%)
- Do NOT just increase antiepileptic drugs
- May want to reduce redundant medications
- Psychiatric meds may be more appropriate than seizure meds
- Gradual tapering with psych team

### If Catamenial Pattern
- Increase dose during high-risk days (perimenstrual prophylaxis)
- Consider hormonal contraceptives (if appropriate)
- Schedule appointments around seizure-high times
- Warn about driving risk during period
